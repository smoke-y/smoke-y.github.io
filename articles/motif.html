"The School of Athens" is a painting in the Apostolic Palace, the official residence of the Pope. It depicts many famous figures throughout history in the Academy of Athens. The central figure represents Plato, who is pointing up, and Aristotle is pointing the other way.

To understand why this is one of the reasons this painting is renowned, we have to understand Plato's Allegory of the Cave. Plato describes people who have spent their entire lives in the cave, looking at shadows on the wall, cast by objects behind them. A man will believe that the shadow is the true form/reality until he breaks free and runs out of the cave. Plato compared attaining knowledge and wisdom to escaping the cave. He points up, in the painting, signifying the quest for the Truth, while Aristotle points downward, suggesting a more pragmatic approach.

"The Death of Socrates" is another painting where you can observe this motif of pointing up to signify the search for the Truth. Socrates is receiving poison, after the state accused him of corrupting the youth and passed a death sentence, while he points up and pursues his students, who are in regret and denial, to never give up on the search.

There are movements against this quest, such as:

2) Absurdism: The conflict between humanity's search for meaning and the universe's inherent meaninglessness and irrationality leaves us in a state of "absurdity". Albert Camus asks us to accept/embrace it and move on with our lives. Even though Sisyphus was punished by Zeus to roll a stone to the top of a hill forever, if he accepted that his actions were inherently meaningless, he would cease to suffer, hence the phrase"one must imagine Sisyphus happy".

For most of human history, this concept has been a depth-based approach. Mostly a 2-level hierarchy, God on the top and humans below. The Greeks have a depth of 3: Titans, Gods, and humans.

The issue one faces here is that whatever belief you hold makes you act differently, hence leading to a different experience. If you are an empiricist, this is a huge problem, as these beliefs impose constraints on your actions and steer you further from the truth. If you are a rationalist, you would also face a problem, as there are countless arguments for each viewpoint of God, and you could spend a long time thinking about and cross-validating the arguments.

Hence, I suggest that all rationalists embrace empiricism until the end of the article, as the problem can be solved in an empiricist manner. I am an empiricist because of the scientific thinking and the philosophy behind engineering, both of which lean towards empiricism. My personal experience and observation further support it. Can we walk down this path just because many motifs support it? I would say yes. The most important idea from the pre-Socratic era is the idea of an atom. How did people from an era without any scientific knowledge that we consider common come up with such an idea? Mottifs. They noticed a common recurring idea that every entity is made up of smaller entities; hence, physical matter should also be made up of smaller entities. They lacked tools to observe and pin down what these smaller entities were, but the basic idea was correct.
